Neighbor disputes

Neighbor disputes have existed since time immemorial. The causes of neighbor disputes can vary, but what they have in common is that they literally make life miserable for the parties involved. The term "neighbor disputes" covers situations where the owner or other user (e.g., a family member of the owner, tenant) of a property acts in such a way that it disturbs the persons owning or using the neighboring property; therefore, a common property boundary is not a prerequisite.

The basis for protection against harassing behavior by a neighbor is the provision § 1012 of the Civil Code, which prohibits seriously infringing on the rights of others beyond what is reasonable under the circumstances, as well as performing acts whose main purpose is to harass or harm others. This provision is followed by legislative regulation of emissions and neighbor rights in related provisions. § 1013 and subsequent. At the constitutional level, it is possible to proceed from 11 par. 3 The Charter of Fundamental Rights and Freedoms, according to which ownership entails obligations and must not be abused to the detriment of the rights of others.

Harassment can take many forms, particularly in the form of nuisance. Nuisance refers to the consequences of a neighbor's actions that affect adjacent properties. Nuisances are listed in the law for illustrative purposes (this is not an exhaustive list) and may include, for example, odors, dog barking or other noise, smoke, etc.

Of course, mutual agreement is the most suitable solution with regard to neighborly relations, but if such an agreement is not possible, the court will decide the neighbor dispute.
